1：What is diabetes?

Diabetes is a serious condition that occurs when the pancreas is no longer able to make insulin, or when the body cannot make good use of the insulin it produces. This afects how your body turns food into energy. In most cases, diabetes is a life-long, or chronic, condition from onset. It may be associated with individual short- and long-term complications. Diabetes can also have consequences with family, and at school or work. More broadly, it can have a cost for health services and economic systems.

3：How Diabetes Works

Insulin is a hormone that acts like a key to let glucose from the food we eat pass from the blood stream into the cells in the body to produce energy. All carbohydrate foods are broken down into glucose which is absorbed into the blood. When a person has type 2 diabetes, their body does not make good use of the insulin that it produces. This is also called insulin resistance.

When this happens, insulin cannot work properly and blood glucose levels rise, increasing the demand for more insulin and putting pressure on the pancreas to produce even more insulin. Type 1 diabetes occurs when the body’s defence system attacks and destroys the cells in the pancreas that produce insulin.   As a result, the body cannot produce enough insulin or none at all.

4：Warning Signs, Risk Factors of Diabetes

Warning Signs：

• Blurred vision • Excessive thirst • Unintentional weight loss • Frequent urination

• Lack of energy • Slow healing wounds • Frequent infections

• Tingling or numbness in hands and feet

These symptoms can be mild or absent in people with type 2 diabetes.

Type 2 Diabetes Risk Factors：

• A family history of diabetes • Unhealthy eating habits

• Lack of physical activity • Being overweight

Other risk factors include:

• Age • High blood pressure • A history of gestational diabetes

• Poor nutrition during pregnancy

• Ethnicity (people of South Asian, Afro-Caribbean and Hispanic origin and some indigenous populations are at increased risk)

5：Possible Complications

Diabetes can damage the fow of blood around the body and the functioning of the nerves. This canlead to a number of associated problems including：

• Damage to the brain, leading to strokes

• Damage to the eyes, leading to loss of vision

• Damage to the heart, leading to heart attacks

• Damage to the gums and teeth

• Damage to liver• Damage to the kidneys, leading to kidney failure

• Sexual dysfunction • Foot ulcers, leading to lower-limb amputation

• Higher risk of infection • Lower resistance to infectious diseases
